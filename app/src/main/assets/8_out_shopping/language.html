Language tips

I’m afraid

It’s very common in English to hear people say “I’m afraid…” or “I’m afraid that…”

Look at the examples in Erica’s story.

I’m afraid we don’t offer refunds on sale items.

Sorry, thanks, but I’m afraid I’m not interested.

Obviously this has nothing to do with being scared. What do you think the speakers mean when they say “I’m afraid…”?

Answer

Saying no

At the end of her story, Erica has to say that she doesn’t want something. She says:

“I'm alright, but thank you.”

It is important to say ‘no’ to something politely. Here are some common ways to do this in English.

Informal

"Can I get you a cup of tea?"

I’m alright, but thank you.

No I’m fine, thanks.

No thanks, it’s ok.

Not just now, thanks.

I think I’ll pass, but thank you!

Formal

"Would you like a cup of tea?"

That’s very kind of you, but no thanks.

I’d rather not, but thank you.

Perhaps not today.

Maybe another time.

Thank you for asking, but I’ll pass this time.

Giving reasons

It is also good to give a reason when you say no to an invitation or offer.

"Would you like to come to the shop with me?"

I’d rather not because…

I’d love to but I can’t because…

I’m afraid not, I have to…

Sorry, I wish I could but today I’m…

Idioms

An idiom is a group of words whose meaning is different from what you would expect. Erica uses two nice idioms in her story.

Just my luck!

I’m so unlucky!

I went to the shop on Sunday morning but it was closed – just my luck!

I’m short on time

I don’t have much time to do this.

I should have tried on the jumper, but I was short on time.
